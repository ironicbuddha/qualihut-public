## The Telling (common, player-safe)

Before there was earth, there was Light and Growth.

Althar, the Sun, burned without form: endless, unrooted, unable to hold what it revealed. Waldrun, the Forest, spread without warmth: potential without motion, life that could not wake.

They did not rule. They recognized one another. And in that recognition, they made the world.

### The First Seed

The Forest shaped the first seed and placed it where the Sun could see it. The Sun did not command it to grow. He waited.

When light and root met correctly, the seed split—not into a plant, but into Being. From it emerged the Wife of the Sun: fertility without chaos, growth with rhythm, life that could repeat. Where she walked, the earth became habitable.

### The Birth of Night (and the Three Moons)

When she emerged, the world brightened too quickly. Life surged. Heat lingered. Nothing rested.

So the Wife turned to the darkness beyond the world and shaped it. From that shaping came three moons—not one, never one—known in later tellings as Elunara’s threefold face, to govern what the Sun cannot:

- one moon rules rest and sleep
- one moon rules cycles, blood, and birth
- one moon rules memory, death, and return

The Sun illuminates truth. The moons make truth survivable.

### The Forest’s Place

The Forest did not retreat. It remained: older than cities, patient beyond kings, witness to every cycle. It is not ruled by the Sun; it negotiates through seasons, decay, and regrowth.

This is why druids whisper: darkness is not evil—it is regulation; life that never rests becomes catastrophe.

### The Fey Lord Who Chuckled

And the Fey Lord looked upon what they had made—light with purpose, growth with rhythm, moons set to keep the dark from swallowing the living—and chuckled. In some tellings, the name of that sovereign is Caernlach.

Not in mockery. In recognition. The story says the Fey did not undo creation. They tilted it: bent distance, time, and certainty, just enough to keep the world alive.

The old Fey saying is rarely translated the same way twice, but the sense is consistent:

Not to ruin the music.
To keep it alive.

The old line that closes the telling is usually spoken, not sung:

“The Sun teaches us when to grow.
The Forest teaches us when to stop.”

Sometimes the telling adds:

“The Sun gives life meaning.
The Forest gives life patience.
The Moons make life possible.”

The story says that laughter fell into the cracks between Sun-law, Moon-rhythm, and Forest-memory. It became thresholds: paths that fold, hours that slip, and names that don’t fit the same way twice.

## How people use this story

- The Solar Church tells it as a warning: growth requires discipline, and discipline requires obedience.
- Forest folk tell it as a reminder: take only what you can repay, and leave the land able to answer tomorrow.
- Rangers repeat the last line before they camp: if the pattern breaks, keep moving.

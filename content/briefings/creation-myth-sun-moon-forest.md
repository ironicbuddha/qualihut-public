## The Telling (common, player-safe)

Before roads, before ledgers, before even names, the world was not _empty_. It was unagreed.

The old story says three powers met at the edge of that un-agreement: the Sun, the Moon, and the Forest.

### The Sun

The Sun did not build the world like a mason. The Sun _judged_ it. It drew the first hard line between what could exist and what could not, and in that heat the shapeless became visible: stone learned weight, water learned to run, and living things learned the ache of hunger and the joy of growing toward light.

But the Sun alone was too blunt. Under pure judgment, everything becomes ash.

### The Moon

So the Moon arrived as the first soft answer: a light that does not burn, a witness that does not command.

Where the Sun makes a single day, the Moon makes a calendar. Where the Sun declares, the Moon returns and asks again. The Moon taught the world to change without breaking: tide and thaw, sap and sleep, grief and forgetting.

Some versions say the Moon wore three faces. That is why the sea still argues with itself under three moons, and why travel is never perfectly steady.

### The Forest

Last came the Forest, not as a tree but as a principle: _persistence_.

The Forest made relationships into structure. Roots held soil. Fungi carried messages. Spirits found places to stand. Wounds became scars instead of endings. The Forest did not promise safety. It promised that life could continue, if it remembered how.

The Forest is why the wild still feels attentive: it keeps accounts in birdsong and bark, not ink.

### The Fey Lord Who Chuckled

When the three had done their work, the world looked almost honest: bright and dark, growing and sleeping, remembered and renewed.

That is when the Fey Lord chuckled.

Not in cruelty. In amusement at anything that thinks it is finished.

The story says that laughter fell into the cracks between Sun-law, Moon-rhythm, and Forest-memory. It became thresholds: paths that fold, hours that slip, names that don’t fit the same way twice. It is why a place can feel _almost_ safe until the pattern breaks. It is why bargains matter. It is why attention is never free.

And it is why the wisest travelers say: the world is made of rules, but it is also made of exceptions that smile.

## How people use this story

- The Solar Church tells it as a warning: growth requires discipline, and discipline requires obedience.
- Forest folk tell it as a reminder: take only what you can repay, and leave the land able to answer tomorrow.
- Sailors tell it as a joke with teeth: you can argue with men, but you can’t outvote the moons.
- Rangers repeat the last line before they camp: if the pattern breaks, keep moving.

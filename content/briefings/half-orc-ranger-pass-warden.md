## Concept: Pass Warden

You are a ranger trained for hard country: mountain passes, faultland corridors, and the edges where roads become suggestions. In a world where maps lie and weather kills more reliably than blades, you are valued for one thing: getting people through alive.

You are not a lone wanderer by default. You are a professional: escort, scout, and boundary-keeper who understands that “safety” is a chain of small rules, enforced without drama.

## Affiliation (known to the party)

You are openly affiliated with an orc contract-house/crew network (what outsiders lump together as “the Orc Nation”). You’ve been seconded as an embedded observer and liaison to an imperial agency team: your job is to help the mission succeed and to build trust deep enough that, in extremis, the Empire can call on orc resources without bargaining from scratch.

This is not a secret. The party knows you report. Your value is that you can be trusted to do it **cleanly**: with witnesses, receipts, and boundaries everyone understands.

## What you do (in plain terms)

- **Route work**: pick paths that still exist, and notice when they stop existing.
- **Escort**: keep a caravan or a team moving without turning the trip into a war.
- **Recon**: go ahead, come back, and report what the map got wrong.
- **Interdiction**: stop the wrong people quietly, so nobody has to send an army.

## Pass culture (what shaped you)

In pass country, access is political and seasonal. People talk about “rights” the way sailors talk about storms: real, dangerous, and not impressed by your title.

You grew up with practices like:

- **Witnessed routes**: you don’t claim a path alone; you claim it with witnesses and receipts.
- **Taboos that keep you alive**: don’t waste water, don’t start fires where wind can carry them, don’t cut the wrong windbreak, don’t shout in a valley that swallows sound.
- **Restraint as strength**: the best escort is the one nobody remembers as a fight.

## How you fit the party

You are useful because the world does not travel kindly. You can:

- keep the group alive on the road
- turn wilderness into information (tracks, weather, animal behavior)
- spot danger early and choose better terrain
- speak “border logic” when law turns into leverage

## Ranger practice: Animal Omen (if you know it)

Some rangers read the land at sunset by watching animal behavior. It isn’t prophecy. It’s the world tapping the glass: birds flying wrong, deer moving at noon, crows gathering without a carcass.

If the land goes silent, you don’t camp.

## Prompts to customize your character

Pick 2–3:

- What rule did you break once, and what did it cost?
- Who taught you the pass-signs (and who still expects loyalty)?
- What do you refuse to do, even for a contract?
- What do you always carry that isn’t “gear” (a receipt, a token, a scar, a name)?

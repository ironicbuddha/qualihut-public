## World State (Canonical Bullets)

_Source: `content/_world_state.md`_

## World Snapshot

Qualihut lies under the paper-sovereignty of the Divine Elvish Empire: a sprawling feudal machine that claims everything in ink and controls far less in practice. Power travels by courier, contract, and caravan; most people feel “the Empire” as taxes, tolls, and the sudden arrival of someone with a seal.

Magic is real, finite, and increasingly _processed_ into something portable and tradable. The result is a world where spellcraft has a price tag and a custody chain—and where money can literally flare in your hands when you try to redeem it.

Travel is dangerous by default. [Old-growth forests](environments/old-growth-forests.md) are protected and politically touchy; the central wilds refuse to stay mapped; the [Three-Moon Sea](environments/the-three-moon-sea.md) is unstable under three moons. Those who gamble on the [Fey Roads](magic/fey-roads.md) can cross impossible distances, but time and memory do not always come back in the same shape.

Time is negotiated. The Empire keeps calendars and census clocks; the Fey do not. Gods are present in the way storms are present—felt, argued about, never wholly trusted. The dead do not come back by default; if a return happens at all, it is a cost, a condition, or a story.

## Current Pressures (Player-Facing)

- In [Hochsilvar](locations/hochsilvar.md), guild power rises and court power turns defensive; decisions feel financial before they feel moral.
- The [Banking Guild](factions/banking-guild.md) is teaching people to treat paper like law—until a ledger hiccups and a riot starts looking like “market correction.”
- The [Southern Union](factions/southern-union.md) smiles through treaties and keeps everyone guessing whether the next insult arrives as a speech, a bribe, or a fire.
- Sea routes migrate under three moons; caravans become targets; “safe passage” is leverage sold twice.
- The [Fey Roads](magic/fey-roads.md) tempt the desperate with impossible distance, and collect their fee in time, memory, and aftermath.

## Campaign Frame (Player-Facing)

- You begin as competent professionals, assembled into a new team under the Empire’s long-view bureaucracy ([Der Weitblick](institutions/der-weitblick.md)) and its cut-outs.
- Expect covert work, leverage, and bureaucracy: maps, ledgers, favors, and deniable violence.
- The world is a sandbox; choices move factions and change the map.
- Magic is high-impact but not free; it has markets, custody chains, and consequences.
- Death is meaningful; replacement characters enter through the same apparatus.

## Leyline Blights (Ecology After Extraction)

_Source: `content/environments/leyline-blights.md`_

## What players would know

People in the countryside don’t argue about whether magic is “real.” They argue about what it does to crops when it’s disturbed.

When a place is blighted, the symptoms look mundane at first: sick soil, strange insects, animals avoiding water, bread that doesn’t rise. Then the pattern sharpens into something that feels like intent. The land starts behaving like it’s trying to say “no” in a language nobody asked to learn.

### Common rumors

- Blights follow “thin places” and old stones.
- Some priests call it heresy. Some druids call it debt.
- You can’t heal a blight by throwing more magic at it; that only teaches it new shapes.

## Mountains and Passes

_Source: `content/environments/mountains-and-passes.md`_

## What players would know

In Qualihut, mountains are not scenery. They are **logistics that can kill you**.

Passes open and close on weather, rockfall, and politics. A road that exists in summer can be a death sentence in winter. Old markers drift under snow, and “safe” routes become stories people trade with more fear than pride.

Those who live by the passes treat travel as a discipline: layered clothing, counted rations, witnessed route rights, and rules about fires, noise, and waste. The mountain doesn’t care what flag you carry. It only cares whether you know how to move.

### Common rumors

- The fastest way through a mountain is to follow someone who knows when _not_ to go.
- Some passes go quiet: no birds, no insects, no echo—just absence. That’s when you keep walking.

## Old-Growth Forests

_Source: `content/environments/old-growth-forests.md`_

## What players would know

In the Empire, old-growth forests are not “wilderness.” They are **politics**.

The trees are protected, guarded, and argued over in courtrooms more often than they are cut. Wood is scarce in the places that matter, and logging is trouble—legal trouble, religious trouble, and sometimes the kind of trouble that has no paperwork.

Forests are also contested memory. Shrines appear where roads don’t, and certain groves are treated like borders: you can cross them, but only if you accept that you are being noticed.

### Common rumors

- Some forests are warded like vaults, and cutting the wrong tree counts as sacrilege.
- The quickest way to start a feud is to accuse someone of wasteful felling.

## Banking Guild

_Source: `content/factions/banking-guild.md`_

## What players would know

The Banking Guild—formally **Il Consorzio della Mano Sicura**—is turning trust into infrastructure. Where old lords demanded coin, the Guild offers paper backed by vaults, seals, and a ritualized promise: your note can be redeemed, witnessed, and made real.

In cities with strong guild presence, promissory notes start to behave like a trade standard. Merchants stop weighing coin and start weighing reputation. In places without it, the notes are still just paper—until someone with a ledger and a militia decides they aren’t.

### Common rumors

- Guild vaults hold unusual reserves.
- Debtors sometimes vanish into “administrative disputes.”

## The Fey Roads

_Source: `content/magic/fey-roads.md`_

## What players would know

The Fey Roads are not roads in the sensible sense. They are liminal paths where distance stops behaving, time slips its leash, and the world feels like it’s being negotiated rather than inhabited. People who travel them speak of “missing time” the way sailors speak of storms: not as a metaphor, but as a debt.

Most sane folk avoid them. Those who can’t—spies, desperate merchants, pilgrims chasing miracles—do it with rituals, bargains, and guides who never quite answer questions directly. The Fey do not experience the Roads as dangerous; that’s part of what makes them terrifying.

### Common rumors

- You can cross a hundred miles in a night… and lose a week you’ll never remember.
- Gates demand payment, but the price isn’t always coin.

## Leyline Convergences

_Source: `content/magic/leyline-convergences.md`_

## What players would know

Some places are “bright” on certain days of the year. Leyline convergences are sites where magic gathers and coheres—seasonally, unpredictably to outsiders, and never safely. Springs run strangely. Leaves change color overnight. Old stones feel warm to the touch even in cold air.

Pilgrims and opportunists alike seek these places when they “wake,” because miracles happen there and so do disasters. Officials sometimes attend without insignia, as if hierarchy itself is a provocation.

### Common rumors

- Convergences don’t like being claimed; anyone who tries to build a fort on one ends up building a ruin.
- In winter, roads “forget” how to reach certain bright places, no matter how well you know the way.

- [Leyline Blights (Ecology After Extraction)](../environments/leyline-blights.md)

## Refined Magic

_Source: `content/magic/refined-magic.md`_

## What players would know

Refined magic is magical power made portable: processed, contained, and traded. Spellcasters use it to push a casting beyond its ordinary limits or to fuel work that would otherwise be impossible. Everyone else treats it the way they treat gold and gunpowder at the same time—valuable, dangerous, and worth killing for.

In much of the Empire and its neighbors, refined magic is becoming a trade standard. Banks and caravans use it to back promissory notes and settle large transactions without hauling bulk coin. Where the [Banking Guild](../factions/banking-guild.md) is strong, it’s regulated, sealed, and audited. Where it isn’t, it moves like contraband with respectable paperwork.

## Campaign Overview

_Source: `content/meta/campaign.md`_

## What this campaign is

This campaign lives in a high-magic feudal empire where guild power is rising, divine institutions are unreliable in ways nobody wants to admit, and “fast travel” exists only through liminal bargains that bend time and memory.

### Tone & themes

- wonder and dread
- political absurdity
- scarcity and trade
- secrets with consequences

### What characters might know

Most people in the Empire know a few practical truths:

- The sea is dangerous ([the Three-Moon Sea](../environments/the-three-moon-sea.md) makes tides strange; monsters treat lanes as feeding corridors).
- [Old-growth forests](../environments/old-growth-forests.md) are protected and politically guarded; wood is scarce and logging is trouble.
- Magic can be refined and traded like currency, which means money now has arcane consequences.

## Elves

_Source: `content/races/elves.md`_

## What players would know

Elves are long-lived custodians of harmony and stability: stewards of archives, forests, and heraldry—people who think in decades and act as if patience is a moral virtue. Their counsel props up noble legitimacy, their paperwork outlasts wars, and their aesthetic sense makes even oppression look calm.

They move slowly on decisions, preferring careful curation over quick action. That slowness can feel like wisdom or like indifference, depending on who is bleeding.

Elves commonly live **up to ~1500 years**. That long horizon shapes everything: debts, favors, grudges, and “temporary” measures that become policy.

### Common rumors

- Elvish bloodwardens quietly veto marriages.
- Elves hoard the oldest maps and songs.

## Fey

_Source: `content/races/fey.md`_

## What players would know

The Fey are dangerous, transactional beings whose courts migrate and whose presence warps time along the [Fey Roads](../magic/fey-roads.md). They bargain the way merchants bargain—precisely, relentlessly—except the goods are often memories, names, years, and the shape of a promise.

People who survive an encounter often describe it the same way: as if the memory itself can’t decide what happened, only that it mattered.

### Common rumors

- Fey deals pay out in madness.
- Time slips worst near Fey gates during moon-change.

## Humans

_Source: `content/races/humans-living-in-the-elven-empire.md`_

## What players would know

Most of the Empire’s population is human or half‑elf. Outside the high nobility, citizenship and daily life are more local than imperial: towns and villages run on bargains, grudges, and practical alliances, rarely unified for long.

Imperial authority is thin, so local lords, guilds, mayors, and caravans negotiate power deal by deal. “The law” is often whoever arrived last with a seal and enough people to enforce it.

### Common rumors

- Every province is controlled by one of the elven noble houses and has its own petty war brewing usually with neighboring provinces over some imagined slight or old grudge or contested resources, access to trade routes and so on the way petty neighbours usually feud.
- Paper notes and guild favors matter more than crowns.

## Humans

_Source: `content/races/humans-living-in-the-human-majority-countries.md`_

## What players would know

In the human-majority countries, most towns and villages are human-run but rarely unified. Alliances form around trade, marriage, and shared threats, then dissolve when the next season’s problems arrive.

Imperial authority (where it exists at all) is thin. Local lords, guilds, and caravans negotiate power deal by deal, and a hard winter can redraw “politics” faster than any decree.

### Common rumors

- Every province has its own petty war brewing.
- Paper notes and guild favors matter more than crowns.

## Orcs

_Source: `content/races/orcs.md`_

## What players would know

Orcs are fully woven into society as caravan guards, dock muscle, negotiators, and fixers. In trade hubs and border towns, they often serve as go‑betweens—people who can speak to guild enforcers and honest merchants in the same hour without flinching.

They’re common enough to be unremarkable and networked enough to be dangerous to underestimate.

Many orc families also come from hard country—high passes, faultland corridors, and the edges where roads become suggestions. Those orcs tend to be practical about wilderness: you don’t “conquer” terrain; you negotiate with it.

In politics, orcs are often the Empire’s most reliable bridge to the places where imperial law becomes a rumor: they understand contracts, witnesses, and long memory, and they take “trust” seriously because it is a resource that can be spent.

### Common rumors

- Every trade hub has an orc crew that can move anything.
- Orc bosses keep books better than nobles.
- If you need to cross a mountain pass without dying of weather, you hire an orc guide… and you don’t argue about their rules.
- Some imperial agents travel with openly seconded orc liaisons—because the Empire is learning it can’t buy certain kinds of access with coin alone.
